Measurement, by itself, just gives you piles of difference. You measured your sleep for three months; you have a spreadsheet. So what? *Model* is the move where measured difference is compressed into a hypothesis: a guess about what is going on under the noise, good enough to act from.

A model is a story so disciplined it is willing to be wrong out loud. It is what lets a creature move tomorrow on the basis of what it noticed yesterday, without having to re-derive the world each morning.

## Key moves

- Modelling is compression with a purpose: it discards detail in service of prediction or action.
- A model can live in a nervous system (implicit), in language (explicit), in code, in a building, or in a ritual. Same job, different substrate.
- Self-models are models too — and the most consequential ones, because they decide what the rest of your modelling is for.
- Cultural models (norms, narratives, ontologies) are shared compressions that let many minds act in roughly compatible ways.
- A scientific model is the special case where the model is openly held to its predictions. Most models in life are not held that strictly, which is why they last so long.
- Better living often means *better modelling*, not *more effort*. Effort spent inside a wrong model is fuel for a wrong direction.

## Where this touches lived life

- Most stuck people are not lazy; they are running an outdated self-model with full energy. The fix is the model, not the effort.
- A new mental model can change a life faster than any habit, because every subsequent habit is now built on different ground.
- "Why do I keep doing this" usually has the answer "because the model you're using makes it the obvious move."

## What we're not claiming

- Not that more abstract is more true. Some excellent models stay close to the ground.
- Not that all models can be made explicit. Implicit models often outperform explicit ones in domains where the dynamics are too rich for words.
- Not that we have a model of how models form. We have parts of one. The mechanism, especially in biological learning, is still open.
