## Three relationships

The three cycles relate to each other in three ways simultaneously:

1. **Developmental progression**: You mature through them
2. **Parallel registers**: Mature agents can access all three situationally
3. **Nested structure**: Each cycle can contain the others

Understanding these relationships answers the practical question: when should I be in which cycle?

## Developmental progression

You grow through the cycles in order.

Most children enter Cycle 2 first. They navigate, they make, they act on the world. "What can I do?" is the question of childhood.

Formal education introduces Cycle 1. You learn to measure, model, manipulate. "What can I know?" is the question of schooling. Cycle 1's analytical stance typically becomes available in adolescence and is refined through adulthood.

Cycle 3 emerges later, if at all. It requires something that Cycles 1 and 2 cannot provide: the willingness to release control. "What can I become?" is the question of maturity.

This progression is not guaranteed. Many adults remain primarily in Cycle 1 or Cycle 2. Cycle 3's receptive stance is counter-cultural in societies that valorize analysis and productivity. You can be extremely intelligent (Cycle 1) and extremely effective (Cycle 2) without ever developing Cycle 3's capacity for surrender.

The progression is also not linear. Each cycle can regress. Stress tends to push people back into earlier cycles: the Cycle 3 practitioner who, under pressure, reverts to Cycle 1 analysis or Cycle 2 action.

## Parallel registers

Mature agents have all three cycles available. The question is not "which cycle am I in?" but "which cycle does this situation call for?"

Some situations call for Cycle 1:
- Diagnosing a problem you don't understand
- Building formal knowledge
- Testing a hypothesis
- Engineering a system

Some situations call for Cycle 2:
- Executing under constraints
- Navigating a known terrain toward a goal
- Producing results
- Practicing a skill

Some situations call for Cycle 3:
- Facing uncertainty that analysis cannot resolve
- Creating something genuinely new
- Recovering from burnout
- Encountering mystery

The error is not being in the wrong cycle — it is being in only one cycle. The analyst who cannot act (stuck in Cycle 1). The doer who cannot reflect (stuck in Cycle 2). The seeker who cannot engage (stuck in Cycle 3).

## Nested structure

The cycles can run within each other.

A scientist primarily operating in Cycle 1 might:
- Have a breakthrough insight while walking (Meander, from Cycle 3)
- Allow the idea to develop without forcing it (Manifest, from Cycle 3)
- Then rigorously test the resulting hypothesis (Manipulate, from Cycle 1)

A craftsperson primarily operating in Cycle 2 might:
- Enter flow states where skill becomes automatic (Cycle 3)
- While executing precise technique (Cycle 2)
- Based on deep understanding of materials (Cycle 1)

An artist primarily operating in Cycle 3 might:
- Marvel at a phenomenon that captures attention
- Meander through explorations without knowing where they lead
- Make (Cycle 2) sketches and tests along the way
- Measure (Cycle 1) the results to see what's working

The cycles nest because each one can be the substrate for the others. You can analyze your wandering (Cycle 1 about Cycle 3). You can navigate toward manifestation (Cycle 2 serving Cycle 3). You can marvel at your models (Cycle 3 about Cycle 1).

## The stall points, revisited

Each cycle has a characteristic way of eating itself:

| Cycle | Stall point | Symptoms | Exit |
|-------|-------------|----------|------|
| 1 | Analysis paralysis | "I need more data." "Let me refine the model." No action follows understanding. | Shift to Cycle 2 (just do) or Cycle 3 (stop trying) |
| 2 | Pragmatic tunnel vision | "Just ship it." "Analysis is the enemy." Action without reflection. | Shift to Cycle 1 (understand what you're doing) or Cycle 3 (let go of forcing) |
| 3 | Passive avoidance | "I'm waiting for clarity." "I don't want to force it." Receptivity becomes withdrawal. | Shift to Cycle 2 (actually act) or Cycle 1 (actually think) |

The exit is usually into a different cycle. The stall is the cycle turning in on itself. The unstalling is the cycle opening to its neighbors.

## The diagnostic question

When you feel stuck, the question is: **which cycle is starving?**

- If you have endless ideas but nothing ships → Cycle 2 is starving (you need to Move and Make)
- If you're busy but unclear why → Cycle 1 is starving (you need to Measure and Model)
- If you're effective but depleted → Cycle 3 is starving (you need to Marvel and Meander)
- If you're waiting for something but nothing comes → Cycle 3 is stalled (you need to shift out)
- If you're analyzing without acting → Cycle 1 is stalled (you need to shift out)
- If you're acting without understanding → Cycle 2 is stalled (you need to shift out)

Starvation and stalling are different. Starvation is a cycle you're not in enough. Stalling is a cycle you're in too much. The treatment is different:
- For starvation: enter the missing cycle
- For stalling: exit the stuck cycle

## Integration

The goal is not to live in Cycle 3. The goal is to have all three cycles available and to move between them fluidly.

Integration looks like:
- Knowing which cycle a situation calls for
- Being able to enter that cycle
- Being able to exit when it stalls
- Running multiple cycles in nested relationship

This is a developmental achievement. It requires:
- Having developed each cycle (you can't integrate what you haven't developed)
- Having stalled in each cycle (you learn the limits by hitting them)
- Having recovered from each stall (you learn the exits by using them)

Most people over-identify with one cycle. The analyst who sees Cycle 2 as anti-intellectual. The practitioner who sees Cycle 1 as ivory-tower. The seeker who sees both as unspiritual. Integration requires releasing these identifications.

## The felt sense

What does it feel like to be in each cycle?

**Cycle 1 feels like:** Curiosity, puzzle-solving, the pleasure of understanding, the satisfaction of prediction. Also: frustration when the pieces don't fit, the anxiety of uncertainty, the fear of being wrong.

**Cycle 2 feels like:** Momentum, capability, the satisfaction of completion, progress. Also: pressure, the stress of deadlines, the fatigue of effort, the frustration of obstacles.

**Cycle 3 feels like:** Openness, wonder, flow, the quiet of presence. Also: groundlessness, the discomfort of not-knowing, the vulnerability of surrender.

Each cycle has its pleasures and pains. Maturity is not escaping the pains but becoming willing to bear them in service of what the situation requires.
